Understanding Mediation

What is Mediation?

Mediation is a voluntary and confidential process where a neutral third person ‚Äî called a mediator ‚Äî helps parties resolve disputes through dialogue and mutual agreement.

The mediator does not impose a decision. The parties decide the outcome together, keeping control of the resolution in their own hands.

What is Mediation?

A simple guide to understanding mediation as a dispute resolution method in India.

Mediation Explained

Mediation is a structured, voluntary process in which a neutral third party ‚Äî the mediator ‚Äî assists disputing parties in reaching a mutually satisfactory resolution. Unlike a judge or arbitrator, the mediator does not decide the outcome. Instead, they guide the conversation, help clarify issues, and encourage constructive dialogue.

The process is entirely confidential, meaning nothing discussed during mediation can be used in court. This creates a safe space for honest communication, helping parties explore creative solutions that a courtroom might not offer.

Key Features of Mediation

  • Voluntary ‚Äî Both parties participate willingly and can withdraw at any time.
  • Confidential ‚Äî Discussions are private and cannot be disclosed.
  • Neutral Mediator ‚Äî The mediator is impartial and does not take sides.
  • Party-Controlled Outcome ‚Äî The parties themselves craft the resolution.
  • Flexible ‚Äî The process can be adapted to suit the nature of the dispute.

Legal Framework in India

The Mediation Act, 2023 provides a comprehensive legal framework for mediation in India. The Act recognises mediation as a legitimate and effective method of dispute resolution and establishes guidelines for the conduct of mediation, qualifications of mediators, and enforceability of settlement agreements.

Under this Act, a mediated settlement agreement has the same legal standing as a court decree, making it fully enforceable.

The Process

How Mediation Works

A detailed walkthrough of the three-step mediation process.

1

Agreement to Mediate

The mediation process begins when both parties voluntarily agree to participate. This can happen before a dispute escalates or even after a court case has been filed. Either party ‚Äî or both together ‚Äî can approach a mediation centre or a qualified mediator.

At this stage, the mediator is selected (either by agreement or through a mediation institution), and basic ground rules are established, including confidentiality, respect, and the commitment to participate in good faith.

2

Mediation Sessions

The mediator facilitates structured conversations between the parties. These sessions typically involve opening statements from each side, followed by joint discussions and, where helpful, private sessions (called "caucuses") with each party individually.

The mediator helps identify core issues, understand each party's needs and interests, and guide them towards finding common ground. The process is flexible and can be adapted based on the complexity of the dispute.

Sessions can take place in person or online, and usually span one to a few sessions depending on the nature of the dispute.

3

Settlement Agreement

If the parties reach an agreement, the terms are documented in a written settlement agreement. Both parties review and sign the document. Under the Mediation Act, 2023, this agreement is legally enforceable ‚Äî similar to a court decree.

If mediation does not result in a full agreement, the parties retain their right to pursue other options, including litigation. Nothing discussed during mediation can be used in court.
